Ludite

Ludite is a gift network.

A year-round secret santa for people who want to be present with each other.

Ludite is a gift network. To become a member, you give. You give your attention to your friends, then gifts to their friends.

A year-round secret santa, where every month you’re matched with a random friend of a friend: someone you don’t know, but should.

The gifts don’t have to be expensive, but they can’t be mundane. A watercolor of a secret garden. A quilted inside joke. A favorite book. Each one sent with a letter telling its story.

The original Luddites were English weavers whose craft was being replaced by industrial machines making cheap, inferior cloth. As their livelihoods slipped away, the weavers desperately wrote letters, signed petitions, appealed to Parliament. They were ignored. So they began smashing the machines, organizing under the name of a mythical figure called Ned Ludd—a young apprentice said to have broken a loom.

Frame-breaking was made a capital offense in 1812. In 1813, fifteen men were hanged at York. By 1816 the movement was crushed.

Today, Luddite has become a derogatory word for hopelessly standing in the way of inevitable technology. But the Luddites were not fools who rejected progress. They were not even opposed to machines themselves; they had been skilled loom operators for generations. They rebelled against cheap inferior goods that undermined their agency.

Technology isn’t the enemy. The enemy is carelessly trading intention, craft, and empathy for efficiency, convenience, and profit. Modern Ludites fight for what the original Luddites did, by giving instead of scrolling.

When we gather, we celebrate these values. We dance. We paint our connections on a wall and find new ones across the room. We give presents and presence. Giving is the best way to receive, so we give ourselves to be ourselves.
